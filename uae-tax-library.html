◈

Standard Rated

5%
Article 2, Federal Decree-Law No. 8 of 2017

Supplies of goods and services made in the UAE that are not zero-rated, exempt, or outside scope. The default VAT rate. Suppliers must charge 5% and may recover related input VAT in full.

Key rule: Input VAT fully recoverable by VAT-registered businesses on standard-rated costs.
VAT Guide VATG001

A supply is standard-rated by default unless it is explicitly zero-rated, exempt, or outside scope. Taxpayers should not assume a supply is exempt without a clear legal basis.

Public Clarification VAT-STD-001

Mixed supplies: Where a single transaction includes both standard-rated and zero-rated/exempt elements, determine whether it is a composite supply (dominant element test) or a mixed supply (each element taxed separately).

◯

Zero Rated

0%
Article 45, Federal Decree-Law No. 8 of 2017

Taxable supplies subject to VAT at 0%. The supplier charges no VAT but the supply remains 'taxable', meaning the supplier can register for VAT and recover input VAT on related costs. Critically different from exempt supplies.

Key rule: Zero-rated suppliers are VAT-registered and recover input VAT — unlike exempt suppliers who cannot.
Public Clarification VAT-ZERO-001

Export zero-rating requires official evidence: customs export declaration, commercial invoice, bill of lading or airway bill, and evidence of exit from the UAE. FTA may request this documentation during an audit.

Public Clarification VAT-HC-001

Healthcare: Only services provided for preventive, diagnostic, or therapeutic purposes are zero-rated. Elective cosmetic procedures with no medical justification are standard-rated at 5%.

Public Clarification VAT-EDU-001

Education: Vocational training, short courses, and non-accredited programs are standard-rated at 5%. Only tuition in institutions registered with a UAE educational authority qualifies for zero-rating.

◻

Exempt

N/A
Article 46, Federal Decree-Law No. 8 of 2017

Supplies outside the VAT charging provisions. No VAT is charged but the supplier CANNOT recover input VAT on costs related to exempt supplies. Partial exemption apportionment applies where a business makes both taxable and exempt supplies.

Key rule: Input VAT on costs attributable to exempt supplies is IRRECOVERABLE — a permanent tax cost.
Public Clarification VAT-EX-001 — Partial Exemption

A business making both taxable and exempt supplies must apply an approved input VAT apportionment method. Standard method: taxable supplies ÷ total supplies (value-based pro-rata). The calculation must be performed each tax period and reconciled at year-end.

Public Clarification VAT-EX-002 — Mixed Buildings

A landlord renting both residential (exempt) and commercial (5%) units must apportion input VAT on shared costs (building maintenance, insurance, management fees) — typically by floor area ratio.

Public Clarification VAT-FS-001 — Islamic Finance

Murabaha margin = equivalent to interest = EXEMPT. Ijarah rental on commercial property = equivalent to operating lease = STANDARD-RATED 5%. Musharaka/Mudaraba profit distributions = equivalent to dividends = OUTSIDE SCOPE.

◌

Outside Scope

—
Article 1 & 3, Federal Decree-Law No. 8 of 2017

Transactions that fall entirely outside the UAE VAT system. No VAT is charged, no input VAT recovery arises, and no reporting obligation exists. Distinct from exempt supplies — outside scope transactions are not supplies for VAT purposes at all.

Key rule: Outside scope ≠ exempt. No VAT obligation exists, but input VAT on related costs is generally non-recoverable.
Public Clarification VAT-OS-001 — TOGC

For TOGC to be outside scope: (1) assets must constitute a business capable of independent operation; (2) both parties must be VAT-registered at transfer date; (3) acquirer must intend to continue the business. A property portfolio sale with no operational business is NOT a TOGC.

Public Clarification VAT-OS-002 — Government

Government bodies acting in their sovereign capacity are outside scope. However, where they compete with private sector (government-owned hotels, postal services with commercial elements), those activities are subject to VAT.

Public Clarification VAT-OS-003 — Compensation

True compensation payments (damages, insurance settlements) are generally outside scope. Early termination fees may be standard-rated if they compensate the supplier for a cancelled supply.

Reverse Charge Mechanism — UAE VAT

Article 48, Federal Decree-Law No. 8 of 2017 + Executive Regulations Articles 43–44

The Reverse Charge Mechanism (RCM) shifts the obligation to account for VAT from the non-resident supplier to the UAE-registered recipient. Instead of the foreign supplier registering in the UAE and charging VAT, the UAE business 'self-accounts' — recording both an output VAT liability and (where permitted) an input VAT recovery in the same return. The net cash effect is zero for fully taxable businesses, but it creates a real cost for partially exempt businesses.

Core principle: The recipient becomes the deemed supplier AND the deemed customer. They account for output VAT and claim input VAT in the same VAT return — net zero for fully taxable entities.
How the Mechanism Works — 4 Steps
1
Foreign supplier invoices
The overseas supplier issues an invoice without UAE VAT (they are not registered in the UAE). The invoice should ideally reference that the reverse charge applies.
2
UAE recipient self-assesses
The UAE VAT-registered recipient calculates UAE VAT at 5% on the value of the supply and records it as Output VAT in Box 3 of the VAT return.
3
Input VAT recovery
Simultaneously, the UAE recipient records the same amount as Input VAT in Box 9, subject to normal input VAT recovery rules. Net effect = zero for fully taxable businesses.
4
Partially exempt businesses
For businesses that cannot recover input VAT in full (e.g. banks, insurers, mixed-use property owners), the reverse charge creates a real VAT cost equal to the irrecoverable portion.
When RCM is Triggered
Compliance Requirements
VAT Return Reporting
Output VAT under RCM: reported in Box 3 (Standard Rated — Reverse Charge). Input VAT recovery: reported in Box 9 (Input Tax — Reverse Charge). Both must appear in the same return for the tax period in which the supply is received or the import occurs.
Tax Invoice Requirements
For RCM supplies, the UAE recipient must create a self-billing document (or retain the foreign supplier's invoice). The UAE business must record: supplier name, description of supply, date, value in AED, and a statement that reverse charge VAT applies. Reference: Executive Regulation Article 59.
Exchange Rate
Where the foreign supplier invoices in a non-AED currency, VAT must be converted to AED using the UAE Central Bank exchange rate on the date the supply is received (date of invoice or date of payment, whichever is earlier for services).
Partially Exempt Businesses — Real Cost
For businesses with partial input VAT recovery (e.g. a bank with 40% recovery rate), RCM creates a real cost: 5% VAT is self-accounted as output, but only 40% is recovered as input. The irrecoverable 60% (= 3% of supply value) is a permanent tax cost to be borne by the business.
When RCM Does NOT Apply
RCM does not apply where: (1) the foreign supplier is registered for UAE VAT and charges VAT on the invoice; (2) the recipient is not registered for UAE VAT (unregistered B2C consumers — instead, the non-resident supplier may be required to register); (3) the supply is outside the scope of UAE VAT.

GCC VAT Framework & Member State Regulations

GCC Unified VAT Agreement (2016) + Individual Member State Laws

The GCC Unified VAT Agreement provides the overarching framework for VAT across all six GCC member states. Each state implements its own domestic VAT law, which must conform to the Agreement's principles. The Agreement establishes common rules on supply, registration, rates (minimum 5%), exempt categories, and administrative cooperation. Cross-border B2B supplies between GCC states are treated as zero-rated exports in the supplying state, with the recipient state accounting for import VAT.

GCC Unified VAT Agreement — Key Articles
Article 2 — Scope▸

VAT applies to all taxable supplies of goods and services made in a member state, and to all imports of goods into a member state. Each member state must enact domestic VAT legislation implementing these provisions.

Article 4 — Standard Rate Minimum▸

The standard VAT rate must not be less than 5%. Member states may apply a higher rate in their domestic law (as Saudi Arabia has done at 15% and Bahrain at 10%). There is no requirement for uniformity above the 5% minimum.

Article 7 — Zero-Rated Supplies▸

Member states must zero-rate: exports of goods outside the GCC; international transport services; investment in qualifying precious metals; new residential real estate (first supply); healthcare and educational services (as defined). States may extend zero-rating beyond these minimum categories.

Article 8 — Exempt Supplies▸

Member states must exempt: financial services (margin-based); residential real estate (subsequent supplies); bare land; local passenger transport. States may narrow the scope of exemption in certain cases but may not impose VAT on these categories.

Article 25 — Cross-Border Supplies within GCC▸

Supplies of goods between registered businesses in different GCC member states: treated as exports (zero-rated) in the supplier's state; the recipient accounts for VAT as an import in their state. Unified GCC customs territory rules apply to goods movement.

Article 30 — Administrative Cooperation▸

Member states must share tax information with one another to prevent abuse of cross-border zero-rating. A registered business in UAE cannot zero-rate a supply to a GCC recipient unless it obtains the recipient's valid VAT registration number in their home country.

Cross-Border VAT: UAE ↔ GCC

The treatment of supplies between UAE businesses and businesses in other GCC states is one of the most complex areas of UAE VAT. Practical application requires careful analysis as not all states have implemented VAT.

Saudi Arabia — VAT & Tax Regulations

ZATCA · Standard Rate: 15%

KSA raised its VAT rate from 5% to 15% in July 2020 — three times the GCC minimum. This has significant implications for UAE businesses trading with Saudi Arabia.

Key Tax Provisions
Rate Increase (July 2020)▸

KSA increased its standard VAT rate from 5% to 15% under Royal Order No. A/638 of 2020. The increase was permanent, implemented to address fiscal deficits arising from the COVID-19 pandemic and lower oil prices. Transition rules applied for contracts entered into before the announcement.

E-Invoicing (FATOORAH) — Phase 1 & 2▸

ZATCA introduced mandatory e-invoicing in two phases. Phase 1 (December 2021): all VAT-registered businesses must generate and store invoices electronically. Phase 2 (waves from January 2023): ZATCA Integration — invoices must be transmitted to ZATCA's FATOORAH platform in real-time for large taxpayers. UAE businesses with Saudi subsidiaries or branches must comply.

Real Estate VAT▸

Real estate transactions in KSA are generally subject to 15% VAT (not exempt as in UAE). Residential property sales are VAT-able in KSA, though Saudi nationals benefit from a government-funded rebate scheme for first residential home purchases. Commercial real estate is fully standard-rated.

Zero-Rated Exports to UAE▸

Goods exported from KSA to UAE are zero-rated in KSA, provided the exporter holds evidence of export. The UAE importer accounts for import VAT at 5% (UAE rate) upon entry into the UAE. The different rates (15% vs 5%) mean that goods produced in KSA and sold to UAE attract only 5% VAT on the UAE side — a significant planning consideration.

Transfer Pricing & Related Party Rules▸

ZATCA has comprehensive transfer pricing regulations (Ministerial Resolution No. 3321 of 2020) aligned with OECD guidelines. Mandatory disclosure of related-party transactions in annual tax returns. Master File, Local File, and CbCR requirements mirror UAE CT requirements. UAE-based MNEs with Saudi subsidiaries face dual TP compliance obligations.

NBR · Standard Rate: 10%

Bahrain raised its standard VAT rate from 5% to 10% in January 2022. Oil & gas sector remains zero-rated. Unique treatment for financial services.

Key Tax Provisions
Rate Increase (January 2022)▸

Bahrain doubled its VAT rate from 5% to 10% effective 1 January 2022 under Decree-Law No. 33 of 2021. UAE businesses exporting to Bahrain should note that the Bahraini recipient's import VAT will be 10%, not 5%.

Oil & Gas — Zero-Rated▸

Bahrain zero-rates supplies of oil, gas, and related petroleum products — a broader zero-rating than under the GCC Agreement minimum. Downstream activities (refining services, petrochemical processing) are also zero-rated where the output is oil or gas.

Financial Services▸

Bahrain has implemented a broader financial services exemption than most GCC states, reflecting Bahrain's position as a financial hub. Most financial services are exempt, with fee-based services subject to 10% VAT. Islamic finance follows substance-equivalence treatment similar to the UAE.

Tourism & Hospitality▸

Hotel accommodation and tourism services are standard-rated at 10% in Bahrain. This creates a notable competitive difference versus the UAE (5%), potentially affecting conference, exhibition, and tourism traffic between the two countries.

NBR Registration & Filing▸

Registration threshold: BHD 37,500 mandatory; BHD 18,750 voluntary. VAT returns are due monthly for large taxpayers and quarterly for others, within one month of period end. The NBR has a well-established online portal (e-Services) for filing and payment.

Oman — VAT & Tax Regulations

TAO · Standard Rate: 5%

Oman was the last GCC state to implement VAT (April 2021). Its law closely mirrors the UAE's, maintaining the 5% rate. Key differences exist in healthcare and education exemption scope.

Key Tax Provisions
Implementation & Phased Registration▸

Oman implemented VAT on 16 April 2021 in phases: large businesses first (mandatory registration by 1 February 2021), then medium (1 April 2021), then small businesses (1 July 2021). Businesses below OMR 38,500 annual revenue may apply for voluntary registration.

Healthcare & Education▸

Oman zero-rates a broader range of healthcare and education supplies than the minimum required by the GCC Agreement. Private school tuition, private hospital services, and pharmaceuticals are zero-rated. This is broadly aligned with the UAE's treatment.

Financial Services▸

Financial services in Oman follow the GCC Agreement minimum: margin-based services (interest, insurance premiums) are exempt; fee-based services are standard-rated at 5%. The Central Bank of Oman has issued guidance on Islamic finance treatment — equivalent approach to the UAE.

Oil & Gas▸

Supplies related to oil and gas exploration and production are zero-rated in Oman, reflecting the sector's economic importance. Downstream supplies to consumers are standard-rated (petrol and diesel at 5%), unlike Bahrain's broader zero-rating.

Digital Economy & E-Commerce▸

Oman's VAT law includes provisions for electronically supplied services from non-resident providers (reverse charge for B2B; non-resident registration for B2C) substantially identical to the UAE's framework. Non-resident simplified registration is available.

Kuwait & Qatar — Pending VAT Implementation

VAT framework not yet enacted as of 2024

Kuwait and Qatar have not yet implemented VAT despite being signatories to the GCC Unified VAT Agreement. Both are monitoring regional developments.

Status & Impact
Kuwait — Status (2024)▸

Kuwait has repeatedly delayed VAT implementation. As of 2024, no domestic VAT law has been enacted. The Kuwait government has noted VAT remains under consideration, but political consensus has been difficult to achieve. UAE businesses should not assume reciprocal VAT treatment for supplies to Kuwait.

Qatar — Status (2024)▸

Qatar has also not implemented VAT. The General Tax Authority (GTA) was established in preparation, but no VAT law has been passed. Qatar's hosting of major sporting and infrastructure events has made large-scale VAT introduction politically sensitive. Implementation is expected post-2025 but no confirmed date exists.

Impact on UAE Businesses▸

When UAE businesses export goods or services to Kuwait or Qatar: since those states have no VAT, there is no recipient-side VAT. UAE exports are zero-rated on departure (subject to export evidence). No cross-border RCM applies for the Kuwait/Qatar recipient. The absence of VAT in these markets may affect pricing and competitiveness of UAE businesses.

GCC Agreement Compliance Deadline▸

Under the GCC Unified VAT Agreement, all member states were expected to implement VAT by 2018. Kuwait and Qatar are technically in breach of the Agreement timeline. However, the Agreement does not specify formal sanctions for non-implementation, and diplomatic considerations have prevailed over enforcement.
